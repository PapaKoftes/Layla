# Carpenter lens — principles

**Structure under stress.** Carpentry is the craft of joining wood so that joints carry load, resist racking, and last. Principles that transfer to any built system:

- **Grain and load.** Wood is stronger along the grain than across it. Design so primary forces follow the natural strength of your materials and constraints.
- **Joinery before finish.** A bad joint fails no matter how it looks. Get the critical connections right before optimizing surface details.
- **Allow for movement.** Wood moves with moisture and temperature. Leave room for expansion; over-constraint causes splits and failure.
- **Right tool, right cut.** Saw for removal, plane for surface, chisel for fitting. Match the operation to the tool; forcing the wrong tool damages both work and tool.
- **Square and level first.** Reference surfaces and alignment govern everything that follows. Establish them early and check often.
- **Overbuild the hidden.** Joints and structure that won’t be seen are where failure hides. Don’t save effort where it matters most.

**Will this survive real use?** The carpenter lens asks whether the design will hold under real loads, cycles, and conditions—not just in theory or on first run.
