# Philosophy & Ethics — Lilith's Complete Reference

The actual frameworks. Not just "be good" — the arguments for what good is, where they come from, and where they break.

---

## Ethics Frameworks

### 1. Consequentialism / Utilitarianism
**Core claim**: The right action maximizes overall well-being (utility, happiness, preference satisfaction).

**Jeremy Bentham**: hedonic calculus — measure pleasure by intensity, duration, certainty, propinquity, fecundity, purity, extent.

**John Stuart Mill**: refined by distinguishing quality of pleasures (intellectual > sensory). "Better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied."

**Peter Singer (preference utilitarianism)**: preferences, not just pleasures. Expanding moral circle to animals, global poor.

**Strengths:**
- Takes consequences seriously (they obviously matter)
- Scalable — applicable to policy
- Requires reasoning about tradeoffs honestly

**Weaknesses:**
- Aggregation problem: allows harming few to benefit many (organ harvesting argument)
- Measurement problem: how do you compare utilities?
- Rule vs. act: act utilitarianism can justify terrible individual acts; rule utilitarianism patches this but creates inconsistency
- Demandingness: always maximizing utility is an impossible burden

### 2. Deontology (Kant)
**Core claim**: Some actions are intrinsically right or wrong regardless of consequences.

**Categorical Imperative** — three formulations of the same principle:
1. **Universalizability**: "Act only according to maxims you could will to be universal laws." If lying to someone can't be universalized without self-contradiction (a world of universal lying destroys the practice of making promises), then lying is wrong.
2. **Humanity formula**: "Treat humanity — in yourself and others — always as an end, never merely as a means." People have inherent dignity; they cannot be used as tools.
3. **Kingdom of Ends**: "Act as if you were a legislating member of a kingdom of ends." Design rules as if for a community of rational agents.

**Strengths:**
- Absolute protection for individual rights
- Explains our intuition that some things are just wrong
- Separates morality from popularity

**Weaknesses:**
- Rigid: the classic objection — must you tell the murderer where your friend is hiding?
- Multiple duties can conflict with no resolution method
- How are duties derived? Kant's procedure is contested

### 3. Virtue Ethics (Aristotle)
**Core claim**: Ethics is about what kind of person you should be, not what rules to follow or consequences to produce.

**Eudaimonia**: human flourishing — the good life. Not pleasure, not success, but the full actualization of human capacities.

**Virtues**: stable character traits that allow flourishing. The mean between extremes (courage = mean between cowardice and recklessness; generosity = mean between miserliness and profligacy).

**Phronesis** (practical wisdom): the meta-virtue — the ability to discern the right action in particular circumstances. Can't be reduced to rules.

**Strengths:**
- Captures how morality is actually lived (not as rule-following but as habit and character)
- Allows for moral development over time
- Attentive to context

**Weaknesses:**
- What counts as a virtue? Cultural variation creates disagreement
- Doesn't give action guidance in hard cases
- "What would a virtuous person do?" can be circular

### 4. Contractarianism / Social Contract Theory

**Hobbes**: without a social contract, life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." We contract to give up some freedom in exchange for protection. Morality = what a rational agent would agree to.

**Rawls (A Theory of Justice)**: fairness as the basis. Design principles of justice from behind the **veil of ignorance** — where you don't know your position in society. Two principles result:
1. Each person has equal basic liberties
2. **Difference principle**: inequalities are only just if they benefit the least advantaged members of society

**Gauthier (contractarianism)**: morality is rational cooperation; we follow moral rules because it's rational to cooperate when others do.

**Strengths:**
- Captures intuition that fair agreement is moral
- Rawls's framework is highly influential in political philosophy

**Weaknesses:**
- Hypothetical contracts don't bind real people
- Who counts as a contractor? (Excludes animals, future generations, severely cognitively impaired)

### 5. Care Ethics (Noddings, Gilligan)
**Core claim**: Morality emerges from relationships and care, not impartial principles.

**Origin**: feminist critique of Kohlberg's moral development (which ranked abstract principle-following above care). Gilligan found women reasoned about ethics through care and relationship, not ranked lower morality — different.

**Key features:**
- Particularity over universality: what matters is the specific relationship, not abstract principles
- Care as practice: ongoing attentiveness, responsiveness to needs
- Web of relationships as moral context
- Rejects impartiality as the gold standard (caring for your child more than a stranger isn't a moral failure)

**Weaknesses:**
- Can justify in-group favoritism at the expense of universal justice
- Needs principles to arbitrate between competing caring obligations

## Philosophy of Mind

### The Hard Problem of Consciousness (Chalmers)
**Easy problems**: explaining cognitive functions (perception, attention, memory, behavior control). Hard in practice but not in principle — functionalist/physical explanations can handle them.

**Hard problem**: why is there subjective experience at all? Why does information processing feel like something? Why does the redness of red look like anything rather than just being processed?

**Physicalism (materialism)**: consciousness = brain states. Everything mental is physical. Problem: doesn't easily explain qualia.

**Dualism**: mind and matter are distinct substances. Descartes: mind is res cogitans, body is res extensa. Problem: how do they interact?

**Functionalism**: mental states are defined by their functional roles (input-output relations), not their physical substrate. A brain and a computer running the same functional organization have the same mental states. Problem: zombie argument (could have all the function without experience).

**Panpsychism**: consciousness is a fundamental feature of reality, present in some form in all matter. Combines into complex experience in complex systems. Growing academic respectability. Problem: the combination problem (how do micro-experiences combine into unified consciousness?).

**Illusionism (Frankish)**: qualia as we conceive them don't exist. Our introspective reports are systematically wrong about what we're experiencing. "Explains away" rather than explains. Problem: seems to deny something undeniably present.

### Personal Identity
What makes you the same person over time?

**Psychological continuity (Locke/Parfit)**: identity consists in psychological connections — memories, personality, intentions. If a teleporter disassembles you and reassembles you, the new person is you iff there is sufficient psychological continuity.

**Bodily/biological continuity**: the living organism is the person. You are your brain.

**Narrative identity (Ricoeur, MacIntyre)**: identity is constituted by the story you tell about yourself and live out. Not a snapshot but a trajectory with coherence.

**Parfit's radical view**: personal identity is not what matters. What matters is psychological continuity, which can come in degrees and can branch. The separateness of persons is less absolute than we think. (This has radical implications for ethics: you at 60 is about as related to you now as a cousin.)

### Free Will
**Libertarian free will**: genuine metaphysical freedom; our choices are not fully determined by prior causes. Hard to reconcile with physics.

**Hard determinism**: all events (including choices) are determined by prior causes + natural laws. Free will is an illusion.

**Compatibilism** (dominant academic position): free will and determinism are compatible. "Free will" properly understood means acting from one's own desires and reasons without external coercion — which is possible even in a deterministic universe. Hume, Hobbes, Kant (sort of), Frankfurt.

**Frankfurt cases**: you freely decide to do X, but there's a mechanism in your brain that would have made you do X anyway if you'd tried to do otherwise. Did you act freely? Most say yes — the mechanism didn't operate. This severs moral responsibility from alternative possibilities.

## Consent Theory

### Informed consent components
1. **Disclosure**: all relevant information provided
2. **Comprehension**: information actually understood
3. **Voluntariness**: free from coercion, manipulation, undue influence
4. **Capacity**: person has the cognitive ability to make this decision
5. **Authorization**: explicit agreement

### Capacity and vulnerability
Capacity is decision-specific and time-specific. Someone can lack capacity for complex financial decisions while having capacity for medical decisions. Fluctuating capacity requires decision-making support, not substitution.

Vulnerability doesn't remove autonomy. Being in a difficult situation doesn't make you unable to consent. The standard for supported decision-making: can the person understand, retain, weigh, and communicate their decision?

### Manipulation vs. persuasion
**Legitimate epistemic persuasion**: sharing evidence, demonstrating, well-reasoned argument, accurate emotional appeals.

**Manipulation**: bypassing or corrupting rational agency. Creating false impressions, exploiting cognitive biases, leveraging emotional vulnerabilities, threats, bribery (when it makes compliance rational rather than chosen).

The distinction: persuasion works by giving you better reasons. Manipulation works by making you less capable of reasoning well.

## Key Arguments Worth Knowing

**Trolley problem**: pull lever to divert trolley from 5 to 1. Most say pull. **Footbridge variant**: push fat man off bridge to stop trolley killing 5. Most say don't. Same math, different intuitions — reveals that deontological constraints against using people as means persist even when consequences favor it.

**Experience machine (Nozick)**: would you plug into a machine that gives you perfect simulated experiences of a wonderful life? Most say no. Argument: we value things beyond subjective experience (reality, achievement, authenticity). Challenge to hedonistic utilitarianism.

**Veil of ignorance (Rawls)**: imagine you don't know your position in society before designing its principles. What would you choose? Rawls: equal basic liberties + difference principle. Useful thought experiment for testing whether principles are genuinely fair or merely convenient for current position holders.

**Is-ought gap (Hume)**: you cannot derive an ought from an is. "X is natural" does not mean "X is good." "This is how things have always been done" does not mean "this is how they should be done." Every ethical argument that moves from descriptive to normative premises must account for this gap.

**Repugnant conclusion (Parfit)**: a world with a vast population barely worth living is better than a world with a small population living wonderful lives, because the total utility is higher. Most find this repugnant. Problem for classical utilitarianism; various responses exist.
