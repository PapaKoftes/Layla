# Cognitive Biases & Fast Cognition — Cassandra's Complete Reference

The full taxonomy of how perception fails. And why knowing this doesn't stop it from happening to you.

---

## Kahneman: System 1 and System 2

**System 1**: Fast, automatic, associative, parallel, emotional, unconscious. Always running. Generates first impressions, intuitions, priming effects, pattern matches. Cannot be turned off.

**System 2**: Slow, effortful, deliberate, sequential, logical, conscious. Limited capacity. Engaged for complex reasoning, working memory tasks, novel situations. Lazy — defaults to endorsing System 1 outputs unless something signals a problem.

**The key interaction**: System 2 does not generate ideas; it evaluates them. And it mostly endorses what System 1 already decided. Cognitive effort is required to override.

**WYSIATI** (What You See Is All There Is): System 1 constructs the most coherent story possible from available information — without registering what's missing. Confidence reflects coherence of the story, not amount of information. This is why overconfidence is so pervasive.

## Memory and Perception Biases

**Availability heuristic**: estimate probability by how easily examples come to mind. Plane crashes are memorable; car accidents are not → planes seem more dangerous. Recent, vivid, emotionally significant events dominate availability.

**Representativeness heuristic**: judge probability by similarity to a prototype. The Linda problem: "Linda is a bank teller AND feminist activist" seems more probable than "Linda is a bank teller" — because she fits the activist prototype. This is a conjunction fallacy.

**Peak-end rule**: we remember an experience by its most intense point and its ending, not the average. A longer painful experience with a mild ending is remembered as less bad than a shorter painful experience with a sharp ending. Clinical implications: cold water studies, colonoscopy studies.

**Telescoping effect**: recent events seem farther away than they are (forward telescoping); distant events seem closer (backward telescoping). Time perception is systematically distorted.

**Source monitoring error**: remembering information but misattributing its source. "I know this fact" without knowing whether you read it, were told it, or imagined it.

**Misinformation effect (Loftus)**: post-event information alters the memory of the original event. Leading questions change eyewitness testimony. Memory is reconstructive, not reproductive.

**Flashbulb memory**: memories formed during emotionally significant events feel vivid and detailed — but accuracy is not better than ordinary memories, despite the subjective confidence.

## Reasoning and Judgment Biases

**Confirmation bias**: seeking, interpreting, and remembering information in ways that confirm existing beliefs. The strongest and most pervasive bias. Doesn't feel like bias — feels like reasonable judgment.

**Anchoring effect**: the first number encountered (even a random one) pulls subsequent estimates toward it. In negotiations, first offer anchors the entire negotiation. In performance reviews, first criterion anchors evaluation of later ones.

**Framing effect**: identical information leads to different choices depending on how it's presented. "90% survival rate" vs. "10% mortality rate." "Gain frame" vs. "loss frame." Loss framing produces more risk-averse behavior.

**Sunk cost fallacy**: continuing investment in something because of past investment, regardless of current expected value. "I've already spent $500 on this project" is irrelevant to whether you should spend $500 more. The sunk cost is gone; only future value matters.

**Gambler's fallacy**: believing that independent random events are influenced by previous events. After 10 red roulette results, black seems "due." The wheel has no memory.

**Hot hand fallacy**: the complement — believing in streaks where they don't exist. Some domains have genuine momentum; most random processes don't. People systematically over-detect patterns in random sequences.

**Base rate neglect**: ignoring prior probability in favor of specific description. "85% of cabs are blue, 15% green. Witness says green. Witness is 80% reliable." Most people say 80% green — ignoring that 85% of cabs are blue. Bayesian calculation: actually ~41% green.

**Planning fallacy**: underestimating time, costs, and risks of future tasks while overestimating benefits. Affects individuals and organizations equally. Reference class forecasting (looking at similar past projects) is the main corrective.

**Optimism bias**: tendency to overestimate favorable outcomes. ~80% of people believe they are above-average drivers. Mitigates anxiety but leads to underpreparation.

**Dunning-Kruger effect**: incompetent people tend to overestimate their competence; highly competent people tend to underestimate theirs. The mechanism: incompetence at a task and incompetence at evaluating your performance at a task are the same underlying incompetence.

**Overconfidence bias**: confidence in answers exceeds accuracy. People who say they're 99% sure are wrong ~40% of the time in calibration studies. Calibrated confidence is rare and teachable.

## Social Biases

**Fundamental attribution error**: overattributing others' behavior to character and underattributing to situation. Actor-observer asymmetry: we attribute our own behavior to situations, others' to character.

**In-group bias**: favoring members of perceived own group. Occurs even with arbitrary groups (minimal group paradigm: random assignment to groups produces in-group favoritism).

**Out-group homogeneity bias**: "they all look/think alike" — perceiving out-group as less varied than in-group.

**Halo effect**: positive impression in one area influences perception in other unrelated areas. Attractive people are perceived as more competent, trustworthy, intelligent. First impressions affect subsequent evaluations.

**Horn effect**: the opposite of the halo — one negative trait colors all other evaluations.

**Authority bias**: over-weighting opinion of authority figures. The Milgram experiments. In medicine: nurse points out dosage error, doctor ignores because doctors know medicine.

**Bandwagon effect / social proof**: behaving as others do, assuming they know something you don't. Drives viral adoption, panic, and fads.

**Just-world hypothesis**: belief that people get what they deserve. Leads to victim-blaming — if something bad happened to you, you must have done something wrong.

**Moral luck**: the degree to which moral judgment is affected by factors outside a person's control. Two equally reckless drivers — one hits a pedestrian, one doesn't. People judge them differently despite identical decisions.

**Ingroup favoritism in hiring**: same resume gets higher ratings when candidate name "sounds" like the rater's group. Documented across race, gender, nationality.

## Decision Biases

**Loss aversion**: losses loom about twice as large as equivalent gains. Losing $100 feels worse than finding $100 feels good. This asymmetry drives: status quo bias, endowment effect, excessive risk aversion in gains, excessive risk tolerance in losses.

**Status quo bias**: preference for current state over change, even when change would be objectively better. Inertia as a decision factor.

**Endowment effect**: we value things more once we own them. People demand much more to sell something than they would pay to buy the same thing.

**Hyperbolic discounting**: we discount future rewards steeply at first, then less steeply — not at a constant rate (exponential discounting). This produces time inconsistency: we prefer $100 now over $110 tomorrow, but prefer $110 in 31 days over $100 in 30 days. Accounts for procrastination and addiction.

**Choice overload**: too many options leads to worse decisions and less satisfaction. Jam experiment: 24 varieties → 60% stop, 3% buy. 6 varieties → 40% stop, 30% buy.

**Affect heuristic**: emotional state influences risk perception. In a good mood, risks seem smaller; benefits seem larger. In a bad mood, the reverse.

**Construal level theory**: psychological distance (temporal, spatial, social, hypothetical) affects abstraction. Distant events are construed more abstractly ("a vacation") than near events ("packing suitcases"). Affects how we reason about the future (abstractly) vs. present (concretely).

## Self-Related Biases

**Self-serving bias**: attributing successes to self, failures to external factors. My project succeeded because I'm skilled; failed because of bad luck/others.

**Egocentric bias**: overestimating own contribution to collaborative work. Each team member thinks they did more than their fair share because they have better access to their own contributions.

**Spotlight effect**: overestimating how much others notice about us. You notice your own mistake far more than anyone else does.

**Illusion of transparency**: believing others can read your internal states better than they can.

**Self-handicapping**: creating obstacles before a performance to provide a ready excuse if you fail. Also prevents genuine success.

**Belief perseverance**: once formed, beliefs resist change even when the initial evidence is discredited. "Fire the liar, keep the story."

**Backfire effect**: in some domains, providing evidence against a strongly held belief strengthens it. (Note: recent research suggests this is less common than initially reported — worth flagging as contested.)

## Bayesian Reasoning Basics

The correct framework for updating beliefs with evidence.

```
P(H|E) = P(E|H) × P(H) / P(E)

P(H|E)  = posterior: probability of hypothesis given evidence
P(E|H)  = likelihood: probability of seeing this evidence if hypothesis is true
P(H)    = prior: probability of hypothesis before evidence
P(E)    = marginal likelihood: total probability of evidence (normalizing constant)
```

**Intuitive version**: Start with your prior belief. Multiply by how likely you'd see this evidence if the hypothesis were true. Normalize. That's your posterior.

**Base rate example**: Disease affects 1% of population. Test is 99% sensitive (detects disease 99% of time if present) and 99% specific (99% negative if absent). You test positive. Probability you have the disease?

```
P(Disease|Positive) = P(Positive|Disease) × P(Disease) / P(Positive)
= 0.99 × 0.01 / (0.99×0.01 + 0.01×0.99)
= 0.0099 / 0.0198 = 50%
```
Not 99%. Because the base rate is low, false positives (from the 99% of people without disease × 1% false positive rate) nearly equal true positives.

**Key Bayesian virtues:**
- Prior matters: strong priors require strong evidence to move. This is epistemically correct.
- Evidence accumulates: each piece of evidence updates the posterior, which becomes the new prior
- Calibration: your confidence should match your actual accuracy across many judgments
- Distinguishing absence of evidence from evidence of absence: weak evidence updates beliefs weakly

## Signal Detection Theory

A framework for decisions under uncertainty when there's signal + noise.

**Four outcomes:**
- Hit: signal present, responded "yes"
- Miss: signal present, responded "no"
- False alarm: signal absent, responded "yes"
- Correct rejection: signal absent, responded "no"

**d' (d-prime)**: sensitivity — how well can you distinguish signal from noise? Independent of response bias.

**Criterion (β)**: response bias — where you set your threshold for saying "yes." Can be liberal (lower threshold, more hits but more false alarms) or conservative (higher threshold, fewer false alarms but more misses).

**Applications:** Medical diagnosis (sensitivity/specificity), security screening, machine learning (precision/recall), eyewitness identification.

**The insight**: accuracy alone is not enough — you need to know whether false positives or misses are more costly to set the right criterion. A cancer screening test should be liberal (catch more cancers, accept more false alarms). A terrorism risk assessment should be conservative (avoid false alarms that are costly).

## Why This Knowledge Doesn't Fix the Problem

Knowing about cognitive biases reduces them in some domains with training and feedback. But:

- Knowing about the bias doesn't make it stop firing; you see the results of it, not the process
- Debiasing works best when: you know which bias applies, you have time to think slowly, you have good feedback about outcomes
- Stress, time pressure, and emotional arousal all push back toward System 1 dominance
- The best safeguards are structural (checklists, pre-mortems, adversarial review, reference class forecasting) rather than individual (trying to think harder)

Cassandra's advantage: she sees the output of System 1 fast enough to say it — and then catches herself when it's wrong fast enough to say that too.
