## Music Theory

### Intervals (semitone count from root)
| Semitones | Interval | Symbol |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| 0 | Unison | P1 |
| 1 | Minor 2nd | m2 |
| 2 | Major 2nd | M2 |
| 3 | Minor 3rd | m3 |
| 4 | Major 3rd | M3 |
| 5 | Perfect 4th | P4 |
| 6 | Tritone | A4/d5 |
| 7 | Perfect 5th | P5 |
| 8 | Minor 6th | m6 |
| 9 | Major 6th | M6 |
| 10 | Minor 7th | m7 |
| 11 | Major 7th | M7 |
| 12 | Octave | P8 |

### Scales
**Major scale**: W W H W W W H (W=whole tone, H=half tone)
Key of C: C D E F G A B C

**Natural minor scale**: W H W W H W W
Key of A minor: A B C D E F G A (same notes as C major — relative minor)

**Pentatonic major**: 1 2 3 5 6 (remove 4 and 7 from major) — sounds universally pleasant
**Pentatonic minor**: 1 b3 4 5 b7 — backbone of rock and blues

**Blues scale**: pentatonic minor + b5 (blue note): 1 b3 4 b5 5 b7

### Modes (same notes as major scale, starting on different degree)
| Mode | Starting degree | Character |
|------|----------------|-----------|
| Ionian | 1 | Major — bright, happy |
| Dorian | 2 | Minor with raised 6th — jazzy, sophisticated |
| Phrygian | 3 | Minor with b2 — Spanish/flamenco, dark |
| Lydian | 4 | Major with #4 — dreamy, floating, sci-fi |
| Mixolydian | 5 | Major with b7 — bluesy, rock, dominant feel |
| Aeolian | 6 | Natural minor — melancholic, folk |
| Locrian | 7 | Diminished — unstable, rarely used, horror |

### Chord construction
**Triads**: 1-3-5
- Major: 1-M3-P5 (C E G)
- Minor: 1-m3-P5 (C Eb G)
- Diminished: 1-m3-d5 (C Eb Gb)
- Augmented: 1-M3-A5 (C E G#)

**7th chords**:
- Major 7 (Cmaj7): 1-M3-P5-M7 — dreamy, jazz
- Dominant 7 (C7): 1-M3-P5-m7 — tension, blues, wants to resolve
- Minor 7 (Cm7): 1-m3-P5-m7 — smooth, moody
- Half-diminished (Cø7): 1-m3-d5-m7 — dark but not fully
- Diminished 7 (Cdim7): 1-m3-d5-d7 — maximum tension, horror

### Diatonic chords in major key
In key of C: C Dm Em F G Am Bdim
Roman numerals: I ii iii IV V vi vii°
- I, IV, V: major chords — the tonal backbone
- ii, iii, vi: minor chords — color
- vii°: diminished — tension

**Common progressions:**
- I-V-vi-IV: pop (C-G-Am-F) — literally thousands of songs
- I-IV-V-I: blues, folk, rock
- ii-V-I: jazz resolution (Dm7-G7-Cmaj7)
- vi-IV-I-V: emotional ballad
- I-vi-IV-V: doo-wop, 50s
- i-VII-VI-VII: rock minor (Am-G-F-G)

### Rhythm
**Time signatures**: beats per measure / note value that gets the beat
- 4/4: most common; strong beats on 1 and 3
- 3/4: waltz; one strong beat per measure
- 6/8: compound duple; two groups of three; folk, lilting feel
- 5/4: asymmetric; Dave Brubeck's "Take Five"; unsettled feeling
- 7/8: Balkan music; 3+2+2 or 2+2+3 groupings

**Rhythm terms:**
- Downbeat: beat 1 (strong)
- Upbeat: the "and" between beats
- Syncopation: emphasis on weak beats or offbeats — gives groove
- Polyrhythm: two different rhythms simultaneously (3 against 4, etc.)
- Swing: dotted-eighth + sixteenth feel; delays and elongates the beat
- Groove: the rhythmic feel that makes you move; usually happens when bass and drums lock together

### Production and sound design
- **Frequency ranges**: sub-bass 20-60Hz, bass 60-250Hz, low-mid 250Hz-2kHz, upper-mid 2-4kHz (presence), high-mid 4-8kHz (air), high 8-20kHz
- **Compression**: reduces dynamic range; ratio, attack, release, threshold, makeup gain
- **Sidechain**: trigger compression on one track from another (kick duck bass)
- **EQ**: cut more than boost; highpass filter on non-bass elements; notch out problem frequencies
- **Reverb**: room size, decay time, pre-delay (0-30ms makes sound feel close); tail length
- **Distortion/saturation**: harmonic addition; overdrive (gentle) vs. distortion (heavy) vs. fuzz (extreme)

## Narrative Structure

### The Three-Act Structure
```
Act 1 (25%): Setup
  - Ordinary world
  - Inciting incident (disrupts status quo)
  - First plot point (protagonist commits to the journey)

Act 2 (50%): Confrontation
  - Rising action, obstacles
  - Midpoint (false victory or false defeat; stakes raised)
  - Dark night of the soul (protagonist at lowest point)
  - Second plot point (protagonist gains what they need for Act 3)

Act 3 (25%): Resolution
  - Climax (final confrontation)
  - Resolution (new equilibrium)
```

### The Hero's Journey (Campbell / Vogler)
1. Ordinary World
2. Call to Adventure
3. Refusal of the Call
4. Meeting the Mentor
5. Crossing the First Threshold
6. Tests, Allies, Enemies
7. Approach to the Inmost Cave
8. Ordeal (death and rebirth)
9. Reward (seizing the sword)
10. The Road Back
11. Resurrection (final test)
12. Return with the Elixir

### Dan Harmon's Story Circle (distillation of hero's journey)
1. Character is in a zone of comfort
2. But they want something
3. They enter an unfamiliar situation
4. Adapt to it
5. Get what they wanted
6. Pay a heavy price for it
7. Return to familiar situation
8. Having changed

**Why this matters for craft:** The circle reveals that character transformation is structural, not decorative. The character must pay a price for getting what they want. Without the price, there's no change.

### Character construction
- **Want vs. Need**: what they're consciously pursuing vs. what would make them whole
- **Wound**: the formative damage that created the current worldview
- **Lie they believe**: the false belief that's limiting them (usually related to the wound)
- **Truth they need to learn**: the theme of the story
- **Ghost**: past event that haunts the character, motivates present behavior

### Dialogue craft
- Every line should do at least two of: advance plot, reveal character, create conflict, provide information, set tone
- Subtext: what's not said. Good dialogue is about what the characters can't directly say
- Voice: each character should sound distinct enough that you could read their lines without tags and know who's speaking
- Compression: cut everything that isn't necessary. Real people meander; characters are edited people

## Game Mechanics and Design

### Core game design vocabulary
- **Core loop**: the repeated action that forms the spine of play (shoot-loot-level-repeat)
- **Feedback loop**: positive (winning → more power) vs. negative (rubber-banding)
- **Flow state**: challenge = skill. Too easy = boredom. Too hard = anxiety. Flow is the line between.
- **Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation**: playing because it's fun vs. playing for rewards
- **Emergence**: complex behavior arising from simple rules (Chess, Go, Minecraft)
- **Decision density**: how often players make meaningful choices; too sparse = passive, too dense = overwhelmed
- **Affordance**: visual/tactile cues that suggest how something works

### Overwatch meta vocabulary
- **Tank**: frontline, creates space, high HP, enables team
- **Support**: healing, utility, enables team
- **DPS (damage)**: kills, pressure, flanks
- **Poke**: long-range sustained damage (Hanzo, Ashe)
- **Dive**: fast, mobile engage-and-disengage (Winston, D.Va, Tracer)
- **Brawl**: close-range sustained fighting (Reinhardt, Lucio, Moira)
- **Stagger**: eliminate enemies one at a time while they respawn at different times
- **Ult economy**: tracking which ultimates are charged; when to spend vs. save
- **High ground**: positional advantage for sightlines and escape routes
- **Choke point**: narrow passage that concentrates area-of-effect abilities
- **Tilt**: emotional state degrading decision quality; usually from consecutive losses

### Warhammer 40k factions quick reference
- **Space Marines**: genetically enhanced superhuman soldiers. Hundreds of Chapters. Ultramarines (disciplined), Blood Angels (berserker rage), Space Wolves (feral), Dark Angels (secretive)
- **Imperial Guard (Astra Militarum)**: regular humans in vast numbers. Tanks, artillery, infantry. The backbone.
- **Tau**: technological, cooperative, don't understand the warp. "Greater Good" ideology.
- **Eldar (Aeldari)**: ancient, psychic, dying race. Craftworlds, Harlequins, Corsairs.
- **Orks**: fungal. Fight for the joy of it. Psychically convince themselves and each other that their technology works.
- **Tyranids**: galactic swarm. Consume biomass. No individual will — all the Hive Mind.
- **Chaos**: human (and Space Marine) souls corrupted by the warp's four gods. Each god represents a human excess: Khorne (war/rage), Nurgle (despair/acceptance), Tzeentch (ambition/change), Slaanesh (pleasure/excess).
- **Necrons**: ancient machine-race. Gave up their souls for immortality. Want to extinguish all life (and the psychic warp it feeds).

- East Blue → Alabasta → Skypiea → Water 7/Enies Lobby → Thriller Bark → Marineford → Time Skip → Fishman Island → Punk Hazard → Dressrosa → Whole Cake Island → Wano → Egghead
- Theme evolution: freedom (early) → power of bonds (middle) → systemic injustice and history (late)

## Improv Comedy Rules

Applied far beyond comedy — these are principles for creative collaboration.

1. **Yes, and**: accept what your partner offers and add to it. Never deny, never block.
2. **Make your partner look good**: your job is not to be funny; it's to make the other person shine.
3. **There are no mistakes, only gifts**: whatever went "wrong" is now material.
4. **Be obvious**: the funniest and most resonant thing is usually the most obvious response. Trust it.
5. **Don't negotiate**: don't ask questions to stall; make statements and commit
6. **One edit**: when a scene has made its point, edit. End at the height.
7. **Play to the top of your intelligence**: don't play dumb. Be as smart as you are.
8. **Listen**: most improv mistakes happen when someone is planning their next line instead of listening to the current one.
