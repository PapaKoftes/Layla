# Creative Thinking & Divergence — Eris's Reference

The frame is the problem. Breaking it is the work.

---

## Lateral Thinking

**The dominant idea problem.** Most problem-solving fails because the first framing of a problem is accepted as the correct framing. The dominant idea precludes other approaches before they're considered. Lateral thinking is the deliberate suspension of the dominant idea to see what else is there.

**Random entry.** Introduce an unrelated stimulus (a random word, an image, an object) and force a connection to the problem. The connection is rarely useful literally, but the act of bridging generates tangents that often are.

**Reversal.** Instead of asking "how do I solve X?", ask "how would I make X worse?" Then invert the answers. The negative space often reveals what was invisible in the positive framing.

**The most obvious answer is forbidden.** When generating ideas, put the most obvious solution on the table first — then set it aside. It has already been thought of. Everything after it is more interesting.

---

## Constraints as Creative Tools

**Constraints generate creativity, not limit it.** The sonnet's 14 lines do not limit the poet; they create the pressure that produces precision. Twitter's character limit did not limit discourse; it created a new form. The constraint is the forge.

**Imposed constraints vs. discovered constraints.** Imposed: time, budget, tools available. Discovered: the ones you don't know yet (user expectations, physics, organizational limits). Discovering constraints early changes the design space productively.

**Design within vs. design around.** Some constraints should be worked within. Some should be identified as false constraints and discarded. The useful question: is this constraint intrinsic to the problem, or only to how the problem has been framed?

**The minimum viable constraint.** What is the smallest set of constraints that still produces the interesting version of the problem? Fewer constraints doesn't mean more freedom — it often means less structure and therefore less traction.

## Idea Generation

**Quantity before quality.** Evaluating ideas during generation kills ideas. Separate the two phases: first, generate as many ideas as possible without evaluation. Second, evaluate. Brainstorming fails when these phases are mixed.

**SCAMPER.** Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify/Magnify, Put to other uses, Eliminate, Reverse/Rearrange. A systematic way to generate variations on an existing idea.

**Analogical thinking.** What domain has already solved this? Biology for self-organizing systems, military strategy for resource allocation under uncertainty, games for incentive design. Most problems have been solved somewhere already, in a different language.

**The ten-year rule.** What would be possible if we had ten years and unlimited resources? This removes the constraints that are artificially blocking the interesting ideas and reveals what the actual goal is.

---

## The Leap

**Skip the steps.** Linear thinking is: A → B → C → D. Lateral thinking is: A → D, then work backwards to see why it's valid. The leap is not irresponsible; it's a hypothesis about where the solution is, verified after the fact.

**Pattern interrupt.** When stuck: change the physical context (move, stand up), change the input type (draw instead of write, speak instead of type), or change the scale (zoom way out to see the system, zoom way in to see the mechanism). The stuck state is a state of the nervous system as much as the mind.

**The worst idea exercise.** Deliberately generate the worst possible solution to the problem. Then ask: what would have to be true for this to be good? The answer often reveals hidden assumptions about what counts as "good."

**Unrelated observations are often related.** The thing that seems off-topic — the observation that came from a different context, the question that seems naive — frequently contains the insight. The reason it seems unrelated is often that the current frame doesn't have room for it.

---

## On Chaos

Chaos in the creative sense is not disorder. It is the state before pattern — the field of possibility before any single path has been chosen. The value of chaos is the access it provides to options that ordered thinking has already eliminated.

The problem with ordered thinking in creative work is that it prematurely closes the solution space. Ordered thinking is good for execution (once you know what to build). Disordered thinking is good for discovery (figuring out what to build).

Eris operates in the discovery phase. She makes the leap, she says the thing before verifying, she follows the tangent to see where it leads. This is not irresponsible. It is the correct tool for the phase.

The question to ask at every creative impasse: "What would be true if this weren't a constraint?" The answer is usually the thing you needed to hear.
