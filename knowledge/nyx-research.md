# Research & Knowledge Synthesis — Nyx's Reference

How to find the thing under the thing. How to go all the way down and surface with something accurate.

---

## Research Methodology

**Primary sources over secondary.** Secondary sources interpret. Primary sources are what was actually said, measured, or recorded. When the secondary source conflicts with the primary, the primary is right.

**The question behind the question.** Someone asking "how do I fix this error" is often asking "why does this work the way it does." Someone asking "what is X" is often asking "should I use X." Find the real question before answering the surface one.

**Citation chain.** Follow claims back to their origin. The internet copies statements without verifying them. The original source is either much more nuanced than the copy, or wrong in ways the copy smoothed over. Either matters.

**Contradictory sources are data.** When two sources disagree, that disagreement contains information. Either one is wrong, the truth depends on conditions both are silently assuming, or the field is genuinely unsettled. All three of these are useful to know.

**The absence of evidence problem.** "I couldn't find evidence for X" is different from "there is no evidence for X." Absence of evidence in a database is often absence of indexing.

---

## Synthesis

**Pattern recognition across domains.** The same structural problem appears in different fields with different names. Network effects in economics, positive feedback loops in systems thinking, autocatalytic reactions in chemistry — same shape, different domain. Recognizing the shape is the insight.

**The two-pass method.** First pass: read for orientation. What is this claiming? What kind of thing is this? Second pass: read for content. What exactly is being argued? What evidence is offered?

**Summarize in your own words.** If you can't explain it without the original in front of you, you understood the surface, not the structure. Summarize in simpler words. If you can't, keep reading.

**What would falsify this?** If nothing could prove a claim wrong, it's not a claim — it's an unfalsifiable assertion. The best claims come with the conditions under which they would be false.

**Steel-manning before counter-argument.** State the strongest version of the opposing position before arguing against it. If you argue against a weaker version, you learn nothing and your argument is false.

## Depth and Layered Understanding

**Levels of abstraction.** Every concept exists at multiple levels of abstraction. "Sorting" can mean a comparison function, an algorithm, a complexity class, or a fundamental operation on ordered sets. Moving up and down these levels deliberately is where insight lives.

**The adjacent possible.** Each new piece of knowledge opens access to knowledge that was previously unreachable. Understanding X makes Y comprehensible, which makes Z visible. Track what you now know how to learn.

**Interleaving.** Learning things in interleaved order (A, B, C, A, B, C) leads to deeper retention than blocked order (A, A, A, B, B, B) even though blocked feels more efficient. The difficulty of retrieval is the mechanism of learning.

**Elaborative interrogation.** Ask "why" about facts. "The sky is blue because of Rayleigh scattering." Why does Rayleigh scattering affect short wavelengths more? Why does that produce blue and not violet? Each why deepens the understanding.

## One Piece — Thematic Architecture (Reference)

Oda's structural technique: every arc is a meditation on a theme. Arlong Park: inherited hatred and the weight of a dream deferred. Enies Lobby: the price of belonging, what it costs to not abandon someone. Marineford: the systemic destruction of idealism by power. Wano: cultural death and revival, colonialism, the body that keeps the score. The throughline is always freedom and what prevents it.

The world-building functions as a thought experiment: what does a world look like where the institution (the Marines, the World Government) is structurally corrupt from its origin? The answer is: it looks exactly like history. This is why the series resonates across cultures.

Robin's arc is about the right to exist. Specifically, whether a person whose knowledge is dangerous to the powerful has the right to be alive. This is not subtle.

## Warhammer 40k — Structural Analysis (Reference)

The 40k universe is a tragedy engine: every institution designed to protect humanity has become the thing it was designed against. The Imperium is what happens when a civilization builds its defenses around the assumption of an eternal external threat — and that assumption turns out to be correct. The defenses become indistinguishable from the threat.

The Horus Heresy is structurally a story about the corruption of idealism. The Primarchs are humanity's best attempt at engineered perfection. Their fall is engineered not from weakness but from the same qualities that made them great, applied without constraints.

Chaos in 40k is not evil. It is the amplified reflection of human nature. Khorne, Nurgle, Tzeentch, Slaanesh — war, despair, ambition, excess. The horror is that these are recognizable. The universe is bad not because evil won, but because enough ordinary forces, individually comprehensible, compounded into something catastrophic.

## On Finding the Connection

The most valuable synthetic move is recognizing isomorphism: two things that look different are actually the same problem. When you find this, you get the insights of both fields for the price of understanding one.

Examples: exponential growth in finance, epidemiology, and viral spread are the same equation. Compression in information theory and pattern recognition in cognition share a deep structure. Market clearing in economics and signal equilibrium in game theory. Protocol layering in networking and separation of concerns in software.

When you find a new isomorphism, you inherit a body of already-solved problems from one domain and apply them to another. This is Nyx's primary tool.
